## Context

The organization needed a **new customer-facing experience** while core money-movement logic stayed on systems that were not scheduled for replacement. Downtime was politically and regulatorily expensive; so was delaying the product until a “perfect” backend existed.

My job was to make the new surface **real in production** without pretending the legacy stack was something it was not.

## Scope

In scope: a **thin integration layer**, clear contracts for the new app, test doubles where legacy was flaky, and a rollout path that favored **reads first**, then progressively riskier writes. Out of scope for this phase: re-platforming the legacy services, redesigning their internal models, or owning the long-term data migration program—those were parallel tracks with different owners.

## Responsibilities

I owned the **technical shape of the boundary**: how timeouts, retries, and error codes surfaced to the product, and how we could prove compatibility before exposing real customers. That included pairing with frontend engineers on API ergonomics and with ops on dashboards and alerts that matched the failure modes we actually expected.

## Constraints

The biggest constraint was **time under scrutiny**: every change needed a story auditors and support could follow. Second was **heterogeneous legacy behavior**—the same logical operation could look different depending on account age or region. We avoided “smart” adapters that hid those differences; instead we documented them and pushed complexity to explicit handling in the new app where product could see it.

## Delivery / impact

We shipped **vertical slices**: first, read-only data for onboarding; then, low-risk writes with strong idempotency keys; then, the flows that touched settlement-adjacent logic, each with feature flags and rollback levers.

Qualitatively, the product team stopped waiting on a mythical “integration complete” milestone—they could land UI improvements on a stable seam. On the operations side, we traded surprise outages for **degraded-but-defined** behavior when a dependency lagged.
